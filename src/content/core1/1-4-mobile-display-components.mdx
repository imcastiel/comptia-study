# Mobile Device Display Components

Mobile device displays are layered assemblies — not single pieces of glass. Understanding what each layer does helps you diagnose display failures accurately and replace only the broken component.

---

## Display Layer Stack

A smartphone or tablet display is made of several layers bonded together:

| Layer (top to bottom) | Component | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Protective glass (e.g., Gorilla Glass) | Scratch and impact resistance |
| 2 | Digitizer | Detects touch input |
| 3 | LCD or OLED panel | Produces the image |
| 4 | Backlight (LCD only) | Illuminates the LCD panel from behind |
| 5 | Frame/chassis | Structural support |

On most modern phones, layers 1–3 are **fused together** as a single assembly. On budget devices, the layers may be separate and individually replaceable.

<ConceptCallout type="tip">
The exam may ask what component is responsible for touch not working when the image is fine, or image not working when touch is fine. Those are separate layers — digitizer vs LCD/OLED panel.
</ConceptCallout>

## Display Technologies

### LCD (Liquid Crystal Display)

LCD panels require a **backlight** because liquid crystals do not produce their own light — they only block or pass light from behind.

| LCD Sub-type | Notes |
|---|---|
| TN (Twisted Nematic) | Older budget devices, narrow viewing angles, fast response |
| IPS (In-Plane Switching) | Wide viewing angles, accurate color — common in mid-range phones/tablets |
| VA (Vertical Alignment) | High contrast, rare in mobile |

A failed LCD backlight leaves the screen dark, but the image may still be faintly visible when a flashlight is held close.

### OLED (Organic Light-Emitting Diode)

OLED pixels produce their own light — there is **no backlight**. Each pixel individually turns on and off.

**Advantages over LCD:**
- True blacks (pixel is fully off — zero light leakage)
- Infinite contrast ratio
- Thinner panel (no backlight layer needed)
- Lower power when displaying dark content

**Disadvantages:**
- **Burn-in:** static images displayed long-term can permanently etch into the organic material
- Blue sub-pixels degrade faster than red/green over time
- More expensive to manufacture

### AMOLED and Super AMOLED

**AMOLED** (Active Matrix OLED) adds an active matrix of thin-film transistors to control each pixel individually — this is how all modern smartphone OLEDs work.

**Super AMOLED** (Samsung's term) integrates the digitizer directly into the display panel rather than as a separate layer on top, making the display thinner and improving outdoor visibility.

<ConceptCallout type="info">
For exam purposes: OLED = self-lit pixels, no backlight, true blacks. LCD = requires backlight. AMOLED = OLED with active matrix (used in all current flagship phones). Super AMOLED = thinner because the digitizer layer is integrated.
</ConceptCallout>

## The Digitizer

The digitizer translates physical touch into digital (x, y) coordinates the OS can use.

### Capacitive Digitizer

Used in virtually all modern smartphones and tablets.

- Detects the **electrical capacitance** of a finger or conductive stylus
- Works with **bare fingers only** — a non-conductive stylus or gloved finger will not register
- Supports **multi-touch** (multiple simultaneous touch points)
- Does not require pressure — a light touch is enough

**Active stylus:** Devices like Apple Pencil or Samsung S Pen use a powered stylus that communicates with the digitizer electronically. These detect **pressure sensitivity** and **tilt**.

### Resistive Digitizer

Older technology, now mainly found in industrial and medical devices.

- Two conductive layers that physically press together under pressure
- Works with **any object** — gloved hand, fingernail, regular stylus
- **Single-touch only**
- Used where gloved operation is required

<ConceptCallout type="tip">
Key distinction: **capacitive** = requires conductive touch, multi-touch supported. **Resistive** = works with any object, single-touch only. A user whose touchscreen won't respond to gloved fingers has a capacitive screen — that is expected behavior, not a defect.
</ConceptCallout>

## Resolution and Pixel Density

### Common Mobile Resolutions

| Name | Resolution | Common Use |
|---|---|---|
| HD | 1280 × 720 | Budget phones |
| FHD (Full HD) | 1920 × 1080 | Mid-range phones, most tablets |
| QHD (2K) | 2560 × 1440 | Flagship phones |
| UHD (4K) | 3840 × 2160 | High-end tablets, some flagships |

### PPI (Pixels Per Inch)

PPI measures pixel density — how many pixels fit in one inch of screen. Higher PPI = sharper image at the same viewing distance.

- **~300 PPI** is the approximate threshold where individual pixels become indistinguishable at normal viewing distance
- A 5-inch FHD phone has ~441 PPI — well above threshold
- A 10-inch FHD tablet has ~220 PPI — visibly less sharp at close range

<ConceptCallout type="info">
Smaller screen + same resolution = higher PPI = sharper image. Two phones with identical resolution but different screen sizes will look noticeably different in sharpness.
</ConceptCallout>

## Common Display Failures and Diagnosis

| Symptom | Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| No image, touch still works | LCD/OLED panel failed, or backlight failed (LCD) |
| Image visible, touch unresponsive | Digitizer failed or connector disconnected |
| Dim image, washed-out colors | Backlight failing (LCD only) |
| Dark screen, image faintly visible with flashlight | Backlight completely failed (LCD) |
| Flickering or color bands | Loose or damaged ribbon cable |
| Dead pixels (black or white spots) | OLED pixel failure or LCD damage |
| Ghost touch (phantom input) | Digitizer damaged — often from impact or moisture |
| Burn-in (permanent ghost image) | OLED burn-in from prolonged static content |
| Touch registers in wrong location | Digitizer calibration issue after replacement |

<ConceptCallout type="warning">
After replacing a display assembly, always verify multi-touch works at all corners of the screen. An incorrectly seated digitizer cable causes offset touch — taps register a few millimeters away from where the user actually touched.
</ConceptCallout>

## Screen Replacement Considerations

### Fused vs Separate Assemblies

- **Fused (most modern devices):** Glass + digitizer + LCD/OLED sold as one unit. More expensive but straightforward — swap the whole assembly.
- **Separate layers (older/budget devices):** Glass can be replaced independently. Requires a heat gun and suction cups; breakage rate is high.

### OEM vs Aftermarket Panels

| Type | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| OEM (original manufacturer) | Color accuracy, all features work (True Tone, Face ID alignment) | Most expensive |
| High-quality aftermarket (INCELL) | Lower cost, acceptable quality | May not support True Tone or trigger "non-genuine" warnings |
| Budget aftermarket | Cheapest | Poor color, shorter lifespan, touch sensitivity problems |

<ConceptCallout type="tip">
On iPhone replacements: non-OEM displays may disable True Tone and show a "Non-genuine display" alert. Always inform the customer before using aftermarket parts.
</ConceptCallout>

## Sensors Associated with the Display

| Sensor | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Ambient light sensor | Front bezel | Adjusts screen brightness automatically |
| Proximity sensor | Front bezel | Turns off screen during calls to prevent cheek touches |
| Face ID / facial recognition | Front camera array | IR dot projector + camera for biometric unlock |
| Under-screen fingerprint (optical) | Under OLED panel | Uses OLED pixel light to illuminate the fingerprint |

<ConceptCallout type="info">
Under-screen optical fingerprint sensors only work with OLED displays — they use the panel's own light to illuminate the finger. LCD displays cannot support this; they require a side-mounted or rear-mounted fingerprint sensor instead.
</ConceptCallout>
