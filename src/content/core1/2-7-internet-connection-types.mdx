# Internet Connection Types and Network Characteristics

Understanding the different ways a network connects to the internet — their speeds, limitations, and appropriate use cases — is practical knowledge for selecting and troubleshooting connectivity.

## Wired Broadband Connections

### DSL (Digital Subscriber Line)

DSL delivers internet over existing **telephone copper wire** infrastructure. The signal coexists with voice calls on the same pair using different frequencies.

| DSL Type | Max Download | Max Upload | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ADSL | 8–24 Mbps | 1–3 Mbps | Asymmetric — common residential |
| ADSL2+ | Up to 24 Mbps | Up to 3.3 Mbps | Extended range |
| VDSL/VDSL2 | 52–100+ Mbps | 16–100 Mbps | Very fast, short range only |

**Key DSL characteristics:**
- Speed decreases significantly with distance from the telephone exchange (DSLAM)
- Requires a **DSL modem** (or modem/router combo)
- **Asymmetric** (ADSL) — faster download than upload; reflects typical residential usage
- Still widely available in areas without cable or fiber

### Cable Internet

Cable internet uses **coaxial cable** TV infrastructure (DOCSIS standard). Delivers much higher speeds than DSL.

| DOCSIS Version | Max Download | Max Upload |
| --- | --- | --- |
| DOCSIS 3.0 | 1 Gbps | 200 Mbps |
| DOCSIS 3.1 | 10 Gbps | 1–2 Gbps |

**Key characteristics:**
- **Shared medium** — bandwidth is shared among users in a neighborhood node. Speeds can drop during peak hours.
- Requires a **cable modem** (DOCSIS)
- Faster than DSL for most residential users
- Widely available in urban and suburban areas

### Fiber Optic (FTTH/FTTP)

Fiber delivers internet over glass or plastic fiber optic cables using **light pulses** instead of electrical signals.

| Type | Speed | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fiber (typical residential) | 100 Mbps – 1 Gbps (symmetric) | Google Fiber, AT&T Fiber, ISP fiber |
| Fiber (business/enterprise) | 1–100 Gbps | Dedicated fiber circuits |

**Key characteristics:**
- **Symmetric speeds** — equal upload and download (ideal for video conferencing, cloud backup, hosting)
- No signal degradation over distance (unlike copper)
- Immune to electrical interference
- Requires an **ONT (Optical Network Terminal)** where fiber enters the building, which converts to Ethernet
- Not universally available — infrastructure build-out is ongoing
- Most expensive to install; long-term lowest operating cost

<ConceptCallout type="tip">
  **DSL vs Cable vs Fiber exam summary:** DSL = phone lines, distance-limited. Cable = coax, shared neighborhood bandwidth. Fiber = fastest, symmetric, most reliable. Fiber is always the best choice when available.
</ConceptCallout>

## Wireless Broadband

### Satellite Internet

Satellite internet connects through geostationary (GEO) or low-earth-orbit (LEO) satellites.

| Type | Latency | Speed | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Geostationary (GEO) | 500–600 ms (high) | 25–100 Mbps | HughesNet, Viasat |
| Low Earth Orbit (LEO) | 20–60 ms (low) | 50–300+ Mbps | Starlink |

**Key characteristics:**
- Available anywhere with a clear view of the sky — rural and remote areas
- GEO latency makes it unsuitable for VoIP, gaming, and real-time applications
- LEO (Starlink) has dramatically improved latency — viable for most applications
- Data caps common on GEO services
- Weather can affect signal quality

### Cellular (4G LTE / 5G)

Cellular internet uses the same towers that provide mobile phone service.

| Generation | Typical Speed | Latency | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 4G LTE | 10–100 Mbps | 20–60 ms | Current wide coverage standard |
| 5G Sub-6 GHz | 50–500 Mbps | 10–30 ms | Broad coverage; improving |
| 5G mmWave | 1–4 Gbps | &lt;10 ms | Ultra-fast; short range, dense urban only |

**Key characteristics:**
- **Hotspots / mobile broadband routers** — a cellular modem provides Wi-Fi from cellular
- Data caps and throttling after thresholds are common
- Excellent for backup connectivity (failover when wired connection fails)
- **5G mmWave** uses millimeter-wave frequencies — huge bandwidth but blocked by walls and trees; requires dense small-cell deployment

### Fixed Wireless Access (FWA)

Fixed wireless uses cellular or licensed wireless signals to provide internet to a **stationary location** (home or business) without cables.

**How it works:** A directional antenna mounted on the building points at a nearby tower or base station. Often uses licensed spectrum for reliable, dedicated bandwidth.

**Use cases:** Rural areas where fiber or cable aren't available but cellular infrastructure exists. Some ISPs offer 5G fixed wireless as a cable alternative.

## Metro Ethernet / Fiber WAN

For businesses, ISPs offer **Ethernet-based WAN services** — the ISP delivers a standard Ethernet handoff at the business, and the WAN is transparent to the customer's equipment.

**Types:**
- **EoC (Ethernet over Copper)** — bonded DSL pairs delivering 10–100 Mbps
- **Metro Ethernet** — fiber-based, very high speed, common in cities

## Dedicated Leased Lines

| Type | Speed | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| T1 | 1.544 Mbps | 24 DS0 channels; symmetric; largely legacy |
| T3 | 44.736 Mbps | 28 T1s; data center connectivity; legacy |
| MPLS | Varies | Provider-managed WAN with traffic prioritization |

Leased lines provide **guaranteed, symmetric bandwidth** with SLAs — used by enterprises and businesses that need reliable connectivity rather than best-effort. Largely being replaced by fiber internet with VPNs.

## Bandwidth vs Latency vs Throughput

These three metrics define network performance and are regularly confused:

| Term | Definition | Analogy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bandwidth | Maximum capacity of a link (Mbps/Gbps) | Width of a highway |
| Latency | Time for a packet to travel from source to destination (ms) | Speed limit on the highway |
| Throughput | Actual data rate achieved, accounting for overhead/retransmissions | Actual cars that get through per hour |

**Latency sources:**
- **Propagation delay** — speed of light through the medium (unavoidable)
- **Serialization delay** — time to put bits on the wire
- **Queuing delay** — waiting in router buffers
- **Processing delay** — time for devices to process packets

**Why latency matters:** For real-time applications (VoIP, gaming, video calls), low latency is more important than raw bandwidth. A 1 Gbps satellite link with 500 ms latency performs worse for calls than a 50 Mbps fiber link with 5 ms latency.

| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fiber | Highest | Lowest | Best | Urban/suburban |
| Cable | Very high | Low | Good | Urban/suburban |
| Fixed wireless | High | Low-medium | Good | Expanding |
| DSL | Medium | Low | Good | Near exchanges |
| Cellular 5G | High | Low | Variable | Urban areas |
