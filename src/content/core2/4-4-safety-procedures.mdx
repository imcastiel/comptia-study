# Common Safety Procedures

Safety procedures protect both you and the equipment you work on. The A+ exam covers ESD prevention, electrical safety, fire safety, chemical handling, and personal protective equipment. These are practical, tested-in-the-field procedures — not theoretical knowledge.

## ESD — Electrostatic Discharge

**ESD (Electrostatic Discharge)** occurs when two objects at different electrical potentials make contact and charge equalizes between them. For humans, a static discharge is perceptible around 3,000 volts. For electronic components, damage can occur at as little as 30 volts — well below what humans can feel or notice.

This means you can destroy a CPU, RAM stick, or network card without any sensation whatsoever. The damage may not cause immediate failure — it can degrade components over time, causing intermittent errors that are extremely difficult to diagnose. This is called **latent damage**.

**Antistatic bags**: the metallic shielding bag that components ship in. The bag creates a Faraday cage that shields the component from external electrostatic fields. Components should be stored in these bags whenever not installed. Do NOT use regular plastic bags — they can generate charge. Place components on top of the bag (outside surface) if no mat is available; the inside is the shielded surface.

<ConceptCallout type="warning">The wrist strap must connect you to the same ground as the equipment. Simply clipping it to a random metal object without a ground connection provides no protection. Verify the ground connection before handling components.</ConceptCallout>

## Proper Lifting Techniques

Server equipment, UPS units, and desktop towers are heavy. Improper lifting causes back injuries that can be career-ending.

**Correct lifting technique:**
- **Assess the load first** — test the weight before committing to the lift
- **Get close to the load** — the farther from your body, the more strain on your spine
- **Bend your knees, not your back** — squat down using your leg muscles
- **Keep your back straight** — maintain the natural curve of your spine
- **Lift with your legs** — straighten your knees while keeping your back straight
- **No twisting** — turn your entire body to change direction; don't twist your spine while holding weight
- **Ask for help** — for anything above approximately 50 lbs or any item with awkward dimensions, use a second person
- **Use equipment** — wheeled carts (server rails, equipment carts) for moving heavy items. Server racks have sliding rails precisely so you don't have to lift the server out to service it.

## Electrical Safety

<ConceptCallout type="warning">CRT (cathode ray tube) monitors are extremely dangerous even when completely unplugged. The flyback transformer and capacitors inside a CRT can hold charges of 25,000 volts or more for hours or days after the monitor is turned off and unplugged. Never open a CRT unless specifically trained to do so. This is a favorite exam topic.</ConceptCallout>

**General electrical safety rules:**
- **Never work on live equipment** — power off and unplug before opening any equipment
- **Wait before servicing PSUs** — power supply capacitors hold charge after unplugging. Wait at least 30 seconds; some large PSUs need longer. The PSU in a PC should never be opened — it is a sealed unit
- **Never defeat grounding plugs** — three-prong plugs have a ground pin for a reason. Do not use two-prong adapters ("cheater plugs") that eliminate the ground
- **Know the Emergency Power Off (EPO)** location — in data centers and server rooms, the EPO switch immediately cuts power to everything. Know where it is before starting work

**GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)** outlets are required by building code in areas where water may be present — bathrooms, kitchens, garages, outdoors. A GFCI monitors the difference in current between the hot and neutral wires. If it detects even a small leakage (indicating current flowing through a person), it cuts power within milliseconds. GFCI outlets have "TEST" and "RESET" buttons visible on the outlet face.

**Voltage hazards in IT equipment:**
- Standard PSU outputs: 12V, 5V, 3.3V — relatively safe
- Line voltage input to PSU: 120V AC (US) / 240V AC (international) — dangerous
- CRT monitors: 25,000V+ — extremely dangerous, even when unplugged
- Laser printers: high voltage corona wire assembly — discharge before servicing
- UPS units: contain large batteries and can supply significant current

## Fire Safety

**Fire extinguisher classes:**

| Class | Fire Type | Extinguishing Agent |
|---|---|---|
| A | Ordinary combustibles (wood, paper, fabric) | Water, dry chemical |
| B | Flammable liquids (gasoline, oil, solvents) | CO2, dry chemical, foam |
| C | Electrical fires (energized equipment) | CO2, dry chemical — non-conductive |
| D | Combustible metals (magnesium, lithium) | Dry powder (specialized) |
| K | Cooking oils and fats | Wet chemical |

**For IT environments**: Class C extinguisher is appropriate for electrical fires. ABC dry chemical extinguishers are versatile and handle most fires including electrical. The critical rule: **never use water on an electrical fire** — water conducts electricity and will electrocute you or spread the fire.

**CO2 extinguishers** are often preferred for IT equipment because CO2 leaves no residue that can damage electronics. Dry chemical (ABC) leaves a fine powder that can damage equipment even if it saves it from fire.

**Lithium battery fires** deserve special mention: lithium batteries (in phones, laptops, EVs) can undergo **thermal runaway** — a self-sustaining exothermic reaction. The fire is very difficult to extinguish, produces toxic fumes (hydrogen fluoride), and can reignite hours later. A swollen or punctured lithium battery should be treated as a fire hazard.

## Chemical Safety — SDS

**SDS (Safety Data Sheet)** — formerly called MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheet) — is the standardized documentation for any chemical substance. Every workplace that uses chemicals is required to have SDS documents accessible to all employees.

An SDS contains 16 standardized sections including: chemical identification, hazard identification, composition, first-aid measures, firefighting measures, handling and storage, exposure controls and PPE, and disposal information.

**In IT, the most common chemicals:**

**Toner**: laser printer toner is a very fine powder. Inhaling toner dust is a respiratory hazard.
- Wear an N95 mask when handling loose toner or cleaning toner spills
- Do NOT vacuum toner spills with a regular vacuum — standard vacuum filters don't capture particles this small; the toner passes through and is dispersed into the air
- Use a toner-specific vacuum (designed for fine particles) or a HEPA-filter vacuum
- Clean skin contact with cold water — hot water fuses toner to skin

**Cleaning solvents**: isopropyl alcohol (IPA) is common for cleaning contacts and components. Avoid inhaling vapors in confined spaces. IPA is flammable — keep away from heat sources.

**Batteries**: lithium batteries are regulated for disposal. Do not throw in regular trash — lithium fires in garbage trucks and landfills are a documented hazard. Use manufacturer take-back programs, retailer recycling bins (Best Buy, Staples), or municipal hazardous waste programs.

## PPE — Personal Protective Equipment

| PPE | When Required |
|---|---|
| Safety glasses / goggles | When cutting cables (flying fragments), using compressed air, working near printers with paper jams (toner spray), working in dusty environments |
| Nitrile gloves | When handling batteries (especially lead-acid or lithium), cleaning chemicals, toner, or unknown substances |
| N95 respirator / mask | When handling loose toner, working in dusty server rooms, working with fiberglass insulation near cable runs |
| Ear protection | In loud server rooms — sustained noise above 85 dB causes hearing damage; large server rooms with many fans easily exceed this |
| Steel-toed boots | When working in data centers where server equipment may fall; required in some data centers as a condition of entry |
| ESD wrist strap | When handling any electronic component outside its antistatic packaging |

<ConceptCallout type="tip">The A+ exam expects you to match PPE to the scenario. Toner = mask and gloves. Cutting cables = safety glasses. Server room = ear protection. Electronic components = ESD strap. Battery work = gloves. Multiple PPE items may apply simultaneously.</ConceptCallout>
