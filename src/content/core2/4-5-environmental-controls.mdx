# Environmental Impacts and Controls

Environmental factors are responsible for a significant percentage of hardware failures in the real world — and CompTIA expects you to know how to control them. The exam tests both data center best practices and proper disposal regulations.

---

## Temperature and Humidity

Computing equipment generates heat and is sensitive to moisture. Maintaining the right environment extends hardware life and prevents unexpected failures.

### Recommended Ranges

| Parameter | Target Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature | 64–80°F (18–27°C) | ASHRAE A1 class for servers |
| Relative Humidity | 45–55% | Tight band prevents both condensation and static |

### What Goes Wrong

**Too hot:** CPUs and GPUs throttle performance to protect themselves, electrolytic capacitors degrade faster, hard drive platters expand, solder joints fatigue. Sustained overheating causes permanent component failure.

**Too cold:** Air below the dew point causes condensation to form on cold metal surfaces. Condensation = water on circuit boards = shorts. Cold dry air also increases electrostatic discharge (ESD) risk.

**Too humid (above ~60%):** Condensation forms on components and inside connectors. Corrosion accelerates on metal contacts and traces.

**Too dry (below ~40%):** Relative humidity below 40% dramatically increases electrostatic buildup. Touching a component without proper grounding can discharge thousands of volts — far above the threshold that kills chips (as little as 30V for some ICs).

<ConceptCallout type="warning">Low humidity is the primary ESD risk factor. When a question mentions static electricity or ESD, think dry environment. The fix is a humidifier plus ESD mats, wrist straps, and proper grounding.</ConceptCallout>

Use **temperature monitors** and alerting systems in server rooms. Simple USB temperature/humidity loggers can send email or SMS alerts when thresholds are breached. Enterprise environments use DCIM (Data Center Infrastructure Management) software.

## HVAC Requirements

### Dedicated Climate Control

Server rooms and data centers must have **dedicated HVAC systems** — never share the building's general HVAC. General HVAC is designed for human comfort, not the precise temperature/humidity ranges and 24/7 uptime that computing equipment requires. If the building HVAC shuts down overnight or on weekends, servers overheat.

Requirements for dedicated computer room HVAC:
- Precision temperature and humidity control (not just cooling)
- 24/7/365 operation including holidays
- Dedicated circuits with UPS backup
- N+1 redundancy (at least one spare unit that can take over immediately)

### Hot Aisle / Cold Aisle Configuration

This is the standard rack layout for server rooms and is frequently tested.

<ConceptCallout type="info">Hot aisle / cold aisle prevents hot exhaust air from mixing with cold supply air. Mixing wastes cooling capacity and causes localized hot spots that shorten equipment life.</ConceptCallout>

How it works:
- Server racks are arranged in alternating rows
- **Cold aisle**: rack fronts face each other, cold air is supplied here (from floor vents or overhead), servers draw intake air from the cold aisle
- **Hot aisle**: rack rears face each other, servers exhaust hot air into the hot aisle, hot air is returned to HVAC for cooling
- Hot aisle containment (physical curtains or doors) isolates exhaust air and forces it back to the HVAC return

### Cooling Methods

| Method | Description | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Computer Room Air Conditioner (CRAC) | Dedicated precision cooling unit, sits on raised floor | Traditional data centers |
| In-row cooling | Cooling unit fits in the rack row, targets hot aisle directly | High-density deployments |
| Raised floor cooling | Cold air delivered under a raised floor, exits through perforated tiles | Large data centers |
| Overhead cooling | Cold air delivered from above | Data centers without raised floors |

## Power Conditioning

Power quality directly affects system reliability. The exam requires you to distinguish between different power protection devices.

<ConceptCallout type="warning">A surge protector does NOT provide battery backup. If the question asks what keeps a server running during a power outage, the answer is UPS — not a surge protector. A power strip provides neither surge protection nor battery backup.</ConceptCallout>

### UPS Types

| UPS Type | How It Works | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standby (Offline) | Battery is idle; switches to battery when power fails | Inexpensive, efficient | Brief transfer time (4–8ms) on switchover |
| Line-Interactive | Regulates voltage with autotransformer; battery activates on full failure | Better than standby for brownouts; common for workstations | Still has switchover time |
| Online / Double-Conversion | Power always flows through inverter from battery; battery stays fully charged | Zero transfer time, best power quality, protects against all power issues | More expensive, generates more heat |

**Sine wave vs stepped approximation:** Online UPS units produce a true sine wave, which some equipment (especially active PFC power supplies in servers) requires. Standby UPS units often produce a stepped approximation (modified sine wave), which may cause problems with sensitive loads.

### Other Power Protection Devices

**Line conditioner:** Filters electrical noise and voltage fluctuations from the power line. Protects against brownouts and dirty power. Does not provide battery backup.

**Generator:** Provides long-term backup power (hours to days) when utility power fails. Diesel or propane-fueled. Requires a **transfer switch** to safely disconnect from utility power before generator comes online. Auto-transfer switches (ATS) do this automatically in seconds. Generators take 10–30 seconds to start — UPS bridges that gap.

**Power strip:** Extends outlet count only. No surge protection, no battery, no conditioning. Never use a power strip for servers or critical equipment.

### Joule Rating

Surge protectors are rated in joules — the amount of energy they can absorb. Higher joule rating = better protection. A surge protector that has absorbed its rated capacity (after a major spike) is no longer protecting anything, even though it still passes power. Replace after any significant surge event.

## Proper Disposal and Environmental Regulations

Electronic waste contains toxic materials. Improper disposal is illegal in many jurisdictions and harmful to the environment.

### Why E-Waste is Regulated

Common toxic materials in electronics:
- **Lead**: in CRT displays, solder joints, batteries
- **Mercury**: in fluorescent backlights (older LCD panels), switches
- **Cadmium**: in nickel-cadmium (NiCd) batteries
- **Beryllium**: in some chips and connectors
- **Hexavalent chromium**: in some hardware coatings

### What Must Be Recycled (Never Trash)

| Item | Hazardous Material | Disposal Method |
|---|---|---|
| CRT monitors / TVs | Lead (several pounds per unit) | Certified e-waste recycler |
| LCD monitors | Mercury (fluorescent backlight) | Certified e-waste recycler |
| Batteries (Li-Ion, Li-Po) | Lithium, flammable | Retail drop-off (Best Buy, Staples, Home Depot) |
| Batteries (NiCd) | Cadmium | Call2Recycle drop-off locations |
| Batteries (lead-acid / SLA) | Lead, sulfuric acid | Auto parts stores, recyclers |
| Toner cartridges | Carbon black, fine particles | Manufacturer programs, retail drop-off |
| Ink cartridges | Various inks | Manufacturer mail-back, retail drop-off |
| Mobile phones | Mixed hazardous materials | Manufacturer take-back, carrier programs |
| Computers / components | Multiple hazardous materials | Certified e-waste recycler or manufacturer program |

<ConceptCallout type="warning">Toner powder is extremely fine and can be inhaled. When replacing or disposing of toner cartridges, avoid shaking them and do not use compressed air to clean spills. Use a toner vacuum (with HEPA filter) or a damp cloth.</ConceptCallout>

### Regulatory Framework

**WEEE Directive (EU):** Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment — requires manufacturers to take back and recycle products at end of life. Retailers must accept old equipment when selling new equivalents.

**EPA regulations (US):** The EPA regulates certain e-waste components under RCRA (Resource Conservation and Recovery Act). Lead-acid batteries and CRTs are specifically regulated. States may have stricter laws.

**State e-waste laws:** Many US states (California, New York, Texas, etc.) have mandatory e-waste recycling laws. Penalties for non-compliance can be significant for businesses.

**Data destruction before disposal:** Before recycling or donating hardware, all storage media must be wiped or destroyed. See: degaussing, secure erase, physical destruction. This is both a security and compliance requirement.

## Airflow Management

Poor airflow management reduces cooling efficiency and creates hot spots even in well-cooled rooms.

Best practices:
- **Blanking panels**: fill all unused rack unit slots with blanking panels. Empty slots allow hot exhaust air to recirculate to the front of the rack, warming the intake air.
- **Cable management**: route cables through cable managers, not across airflow paths. Cables blocking the front or rear of equipment restrict airflow.
- **Proper rack fill**: place heavier/hotter equipment at the bottom; leave some space between high-heat items.
- **Seal floor penetrations**: in raised-floor data centers, cable cutouts and unsealed openings allow cold air to escape without reaching equipment.
- **Side-to-side vs front-to-back airflow**: know which direction your equipment moves air and orient accordingly in the rack.
